# Design principles

The skill does not produce the design. The founder builds the deck in their tool of choice. But the skill's plan-mode and critique-mode output should reflect a few non-negotiable design principles.

These principles apply to almost every deck. The exceptions are rare.

---

## Less is more

The single most important design principle. A slide with one strong line and lots of whitespace beats a slide stuffed with bullets.

In practice:

- One headline per slide.
- Three bullet points maximum per slide. Often fewer is better.
- One image, not a collage.
- One chart, not three.

If a slide feels crowded, it is. Cut.

---

## Use brand colours, not a rainbow

Two or three brand colours, used consistently across every slide. No rainbow palettes. No different colour scheme per slide.

If the founder does not yet have brand colours:

- Suggest a simple palette of two colours plus black and white.
- One primary colour for emphasis (titles, key numbers, charts).
- One secondary colour for supporting elements.
- Black for body text. White for backgrounds (or vice versa for dark decks, but pick one and stick with it).

Common colour mistakes:

- Different background colours per slide.
- Highlighting words in three different colours on the same slide.
- Using red for everything (reads as warning or error).
- Using neon or fluorescent colours that do not project well.

---

## No animations

Nobody is asking for slide transitions, fly-ins, fade-outs, or motion effects. They distract from the content and break the flow when pitching live. They look amateurish in an emailed deck.

The exceptions:

- A short looping product demo video on the demo slide is fine.
- Simple transitions between slides (the default cut) are fine; what to avoid is per-element animation.
- A chart that builds up data points one at a time during a live pitch can occasionally help, but use sparingly. Default to no animation.

---

## Typography

One or two fonts, used consistently across the deck.

- One font for headings, one for body. Or one font used in different weights and sizes.
- Stick to system fonts or well-known fonts. Custom fonts often fail to render correctly when the deck is shared as a file.
- Body text minimum 24pt for live pitch. Smaller is unreadable from the back of a room.
- Body text minimum 18pt for emailed deck. Anything smaller signals that the slide is overcrowded.
- Headings should be visibly larger than body text. Default to 36-48pt for slide titles.

Font choice mistakes:

- Multiple decorative fonts.
- Using all caps for body text. Hard to read.
- Italics on entire paragraphs. Hard to read.
- Underlining anything that is not a hyperlink.

---

## Whitespace

Whitespace is not wasted space. It is part of the design.

- Leave generous margins around every slide. Do not push content to the edges.
- Leave space between elements. A heading touching a paragraph touching a chart looks chaotic.
- A slide with a single line in the centre and white space around it is often stronger than a slide with three bullets.

If the founder is using a tool that snaps to a grid, use the grid. If not, eyeball it but err on the side of more space.

---

## Charts and visuals

Every chart on every slide must be readable in two seconds. If the reader has to study it, the chart has failed.

In practice:

- Strip everything that is not essential. No gridlines, no chart borders, no excessive labels.
- Label the axes only if the units are non-obvious.
- Show the trend, not the data. A line going up and to the right with two endpoint labels beats a chart with 30 data points.
- For traction charts, label the start and end points clearly. If the y-axis is revenue, say "Revenue (MRR)." If the x-axis is time, label months or quarters.
- Use brand colours, not the default chart colours from PowerPoint or Google Slides.

For images:

- Use original photos or product screenshots wherever possible.
- If using stock images, choose ones that look real, not staged.
- Avoid generic images: handshakes, lightbulbs, world globes, hands on keyboards, abstract network diagrams.
- The image should be relevant to the slide. Decorative images add nothing.

---

## Diagrams are little mazes

Workflow diagrams, system architecture diagrams, and "how it works" flowcharts are common on pitch decks and almost always a mistake. Every box and arrow adds time to comprehension. The audience has to trace the path before they get to the point.

There is a principle from human-computer interaction called Hick's Law: every choice or piece of information added to a decision increases the time to process it. Diagrams stack information. Each box is one more thing for the audience to read, and each arrow is one more relationship to interpret.

For a live pitch, avoid diagrams. The audience does not have time to study them. Replace a workflow diagram with a numbered list of three steps. Replace a system diagram with a one-line description.

For an emailed deck, simple diagrams (three or four boxes maximum) can work if they genuinely clarify something. But ask: does the diagram explain it faster than a sentence would? If not, cut it.

The goal is for the audience to immediately understand why the company is interesting. The path to that understanding should be straight and short.

---

## When to deliberately overwhelm

Almost every design rule in this skill is about reducing visual noise. There is one exception: when the point of the slide is the noise itself.

Examples:

- A slide showing every competitor or every alternative in a fragmented market, to make the point that the market is chaotic and the company is consolidating it.
- A slide showing every step in a manual workflow the company is replacing, to make the point that the current process is unwieldy.
- A slide showing every regulation or rule the company has had to comply with, to make the point that the moat is real.

In each case, the visual density is doing the work. The audience does not need to read every logo or every step; they need to feel the volume. These slides are rare and should be used sparingly. If a slide is dense for any other reason, the rule still stands: cut it.

---

## Layout consistency

Every slide should look like it belongs to the same deck.

- Same font.
- Same colour palette.
- Same heading position on every slide.
- Same logo position on every slide (usually a small mark in the corner).
- Same body text style.

If slide 3 has a heading at the top in dark blue and slide 7 has a heading on the left in red, the deck looks like it was assembled by three different people.

---

## What the slide aspect ratio should be

Default to 16:9 widescreen. It is the standard for presentations and matches most modern displays.

Use 4:3 only if the venue requires it (some older projector setups). Ask if you do not know.

Do not use unusual aspect ratios. Tall portrait slides work for social media but not for pitch decks.

---

## Dark mode vs. light mode

Either is fine. Pick one and stick with it for the whole deck.

- Light mode (white background, dark text) is the default and works in most situations.
- Dark mode (dark background, light text) works for product-led companies, gaming, design-heavy companies, and live pitches in dim rooms.
- Mixing both within a deck looks broken.

---

## Logos and trust signals

If the deck includes customer logos, partner logos, or "as seen in" press logos:

- Use the official logo files, with correct colours and proportions. Do not pull from a screenshot.
- Display them in a single row or grid with consistent sizing. Do not let one logo be twice the size of others.
- For customer logos, only include companies that are real customers. Do not include companies that are "in talks" or "evaluating." Investors check.
- For press logos, only include coverage that is substantive. A passing mention in a listicle is not coverage.

For mentor and advisor logos at pre-seed: see the rule in `slide-by-slide.md`. Default is to leave them off.

---

## File format and sharing

Most pitch decks are shared as PDF, not as the original file (PowerPoint, Keynote, Google Slides).

- PDF preserves the design across all devices.
- The original file format risks fonts, colours, and animations rendering differently on the recipient's machine.
- Export to PDF before sharing. Check the PDF before sending to make sure nothing has shifted.

For a live pitch, present from the original file (so animations and embedded videos work). Share the PDF version with the audience afterwards.

---

## Common design mistakes to avoid

- Using PowerPoint's default theme (looks like a 2005 corporate deck).
- Slide masters that put the company logo in the bottom corner of every slide. One small logo on the cover slide is fine. Repeating it on every slide is noise.
- Page numbers on every slide. Not needed.
- "Confidential" watermarks across every slide. Not needed for most situations and clutters the design.
- Text laid over a busy photo. Either crop the photo or use a solid background behind the text.
- Centring everything on every slide. Some content reads better left-aligned.
- Stretched logos and images. Always preserve aspect ratio.
