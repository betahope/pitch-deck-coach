# Audiences and asks

Different audiences require different decks. The biggest variable is the ask. What you are asking for changes based on who you are pitching to.

This file covers the common audiences and how the ask differs for each.

---

## Investors

The most common audience for a pitch deck. Includes pre-seed angels, seed funds, accelerator funds (post-acceptance), and later-stage VCs.

**The ask is specific.**

A good investor ask includes:

- The amount you are raising.
- The round structure (SAFE, priced equity, convertible note).
- The runway it buys (in months).
- The milestones it gets you to.
- Lead status if relevant.

**Example:** "We are raising $1.5M on a $10M post-money cap. The round buys us 18 months of runway and gets us to $2M ARR with 6 enterprise customers. We have a $500K commitment from [investor name, only if confirmed and the founder has permission]; raising $1M more."

**Common mistakes on investor asks:**

- "We are raising a seed round." Too vague.
- "We are raising as much as we can." Investors want to see that the founder has thought about the right amount.
- Asking for an amount that does not match the stage. A pre-seed ask of $5M with no traction usually signals the founder has not done their research on the round size.
- Listing use of funds as a generic pie chart. Investors care about what milestones the round hits, not the percentage going to engineering vs. marketing.
- Naming an investor as committed when they are not. Verify before including.

## Accelerators, incubators, and pre-accelerators

The deck is usually for an application or interview, not a pitch for funding (the funding terms are usually fixed by the program).

**The ask is different.**

For an accelerator, the founder is asking to be accepted into the program. The deck should answer:

- Why is this team a fit for this specific program?
- What will the team get out of the program (mentorship, network, focus, capital)?
- What will the team commit to during the program (relocation, full-time, etc.)?

The "ask" slide for an accelerator is usually a closing slide that reinforces fit, not a financial ask. Some accelerators expect a brief mention of any post-program funding plans, but the lead is fit, not money.

**Common mistakes on accelerator decks:**

- Treating it as an investor pitch. The accelerator is not deciding whether to invest in this company in isolation; they are deciding whether to accept it into a cohort.
- Generic "why this program" answers. Accelerators read hundreds of applications. Generic answers signal the founder is applying everywhere.
- Inflated traction. Accelerators verify.

For a pre-accelerator (where the company is even earlier), traction is often replaced by evidence of customer discovery, named willingness-to-pay conversations, and team commitment.

For more on accelerator applications generally, see the startup-application-coach skill (a separate skill that covers the written application form).

## Demo days

A demo day is the live pitch event at the end of an accelerator program. The audience is investors and program partners. The ask is back to the investor ask: how much, on what terms, for what milestones.

Demo day decks are almost always live pitch decks. Length is usually 3 minutes. See `deck-types.md` for the 3-minute pitch structure.

**Common mistakes on demo day decks:**

- Going over time. Demo day audiences see 10-20 pitches in a row. Going over signals lack of preparation.
- Pitching in pairs. One founder pitches the deck, even if there are co-founders on stage.
- Reading from the slides. The slides support the founder; the founder is the message.

## Customers (B2B)

Some pitch decks are not for funding. They are for selling to a customer.

**The ask is to win the customer.**

A customer pitch deck is more like a sales deck than an investor deck. It usually includes:

- The customer's problem (often more specific than an investor pitch, framed in the customer's language).
- The solution and how it fits into the customer's workflow.
- A demo or case study.
- Pricing and contract terms.
- Implementation and support details.
- Next steps. The ask is usually "let's run a pilot" or "let's set up a procurement conversation."

**Common mistakes on customer decks:**

- Including investor-pitch material (TAM, fundraising history). The customer does not care.
- Naming other customers without permission.
- Vague pricing that forces the customer to ask. If pricing depends on the customer's profile, say so and explain the model.

This skill is primarily focused on investor and accelerator decks, but the same principles apply. If the founder is building a customer deck, ask what the buyer's role is and what stage of the sales process the deck is for. The deck for a first meeting is different from the deck for a procurement review.

## Partners

A partner pitch deck is for a strategic partner, channel partner, or distribution partner.

**The ask is the partnership.**

The deck usually includes:

- Why this partnership makes sense for both sides.
- What the company brings to the partnership.
- What the company is asking the partner to commit to.
- Examples of similar partnerships if relevant.
- Specific next steps.

The principles overlap with the customer deck. Ask the founder what the partner gets out of it; that should be the lead.

---

## Internal stakeholders

Some pitch decks are internal: pitching the board, pitching co-founders, pitching the team. These are rare for early-stage startups but happen at later stages.

The ask depends on the audience. Be specific. Internal decks should be the most direct of all because the audience already has context.

---

## Mixed audiences

Sometimes the same deck has to work for multiple audiences. This is usually a sign that the founder has not made a clear decision about which deck this is.

The right answer is usually two decks. A short live-pitch deck for events and meetings. A longer emailed deck for follow-up. They share content but are different artefacts.

If the founder insists on a single deck, lean towards the emailed format (denser slides, narrative titles) and accept that it will not work as well for live pitching as a purpose-built live deck would.

---

## How to figure out the right ask

Before drafting the ask slide, ask the founder:

1. Who is the audience? Be specific. "Investors" is too broad. "Pre-seed angels in Europe" is more useful.
2. What do you want them to do after seeing this deck? Schedule a meeting? Send a term sheet? Sign a contract? Accept us into the program?
3. What is the specific commitment you are asking them to make?
4. What proof do you have that you have earned the right to make this ask?

The answers shape the ask slide and often the closing slide as well.
