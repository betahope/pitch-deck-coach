# Slide-by-slide guide

This file covers the slides that commonly appear in startup pitch decks. For each: what the slide is for, what good looks like, what to avoid, and weak vs. strong examples.

The slides are listed in default order. Adjust based on length, audience, and what the founder has. See `SKILL.md` for the default order and adaptations.

For deck length and emailed vs. live distinctions, see `deck-types.md`. For audience-specific asks, see `audience-asks.md`.

---

## Cover / intro slide

**What it is for:** Tell the audience who you are and what the company does in the time it takes to read the slide.

**What good looks like:**
- Company name and logo.
- One-line description of what the company does. Matter of fact. Not a tagline, not a marketing line.
- Founder names (and titles if relevant).
- Contact email or website.
- For a live pitch, the date and event are sometimes included as a small footer.

**What to avoid:**
- Vague taglines like "Reimagining the future of X."
- Mission statements on the cover. Save those for later if they belong at all.
- Long paragraphs of context.
- Stock images of handshakes, lightbulbs, or city skylines.

**Weak:** "Acme: The future of intelligent commerce."

**Strong:** "Acme: A pricing tool for ecommerce stores that updates prices automatically based on competitor data."

## Problem slide

**What it is for:** Tell the audience who has the problem, what it costs them, and why current solutions are not good enough.

**What good looks like:**
- Specific to a defined group of people, not "everyone."
- Quantifies the pain where possible (cost, time, frequency, impact).
- Describes what those people currently do and why it fails them.
- Does not describe the solution. Only the problem.

**What to avoid:**
- "There is no AI-powered X." This is the solution disguised as a problem.
- Vague scale claims. "This is a huge problem." Why is it huge? Be specific.
- Widening the target group to make the problem sound bigger.
- Listing three problems instead of one. One sharp problem beats three blurry ones.

**Weak:** "Restaurants struggle with health and safety."

**Strong:** "Mice droppings are the leading cause for restaurant owners in New York to receive a C or lower health grade. Once a low grade is posted, revenue drops 80% and 7 of 10 restaurants close within 30 days."

## Solution slide

**What it is for:** Describe what you built, mechanically. What it actually does. How it works.

**What good looks like:**
- Matter of fact description of what the product is.
- Explains what it does AND how it does it, not just what category it is in.
- A reader could roughly reproduce the product from the description.
- Often works well with a comparison ("it is like X but for Y").

**What to avoid:**
- Marketing language ("revolutionary", "innovative", "disruptive").
- Vague category claims ("a platform for...").
- Stacking buzzwords (AI + blockchain + IoT).
- Describing what the product could become rather than what it is now.

**Weak:** "An innovative AI-powered platform that revolutionises restaurant pest control."

**Strong:** "Mousetrap AI uses cameras and motion sensors to identify a mouse and trigger a baited trap. When the trap fires, we send the restaurant a text and dispatch a technician to remove the mouse within two hours."

## Demo slide

**What it is for:** Show the product working. This is the most important slide if you have a product.

**What good looks like:**
- A live demo (in person), or a short looping video (in deck), or three to five clear screenshots.
- Shows the product solving a specific use case for a specific user, not a feature tour.
- For an emailed deck, includes brief captions explaining what is happening on each screen.
- Gets to the demo fast. Do not bury it after market sizing or competition slides.

**What to avoid:**
- Feature tours. "Here is the dashboard, here is the settings panel, here is the export button." Tells the viewer nothing.
- Long videos. Two minutes is too long. Thirty seconds is plenty.
- Polished marketing video that hides the actual product behind animation.
- Demo videos with voiceover from a hired narrator. The founder's own voice is fine.

**A note on screenshots.** Raw product screenshots almost always fail the legible / simple / obvious test, especially on live-pitch slides. The text in most interfaces is too small to read from the back of a room. Most interfaces do multiple things at once, so the audience does not know where to look. Most screenshots take longer than a glance to understand. For a live pitch, prefer one of:

- A short looping video (under 30 seconds) of the product solving a specific use case.
- A simplified workflow shown as a numbered list of steps. For example: "1. Choose amount. 2. Choose stock. 3. Send." A list like this often explains the product better than a screenshot of the actual interface.
- One zoomed-in screenshot of a single critical moment, with a caption explaining what is happening.

For an emailed deck, screenshots are more workable because the reader can study them, but they still need captions and they still need to be cropped to the part of the screen that matters. Do not paste a full UI screenshot and expect the reader to find the point.

**Weak (feature tour):** "This is our dashboard. You can filter by date, export to CSV, share with your team."

**Strong (use case):** "Maria is a hiring manager at a 50-person company. She gets 200 applications per role and spends 10 hours a week sorting them. She uploads the job description; 30 seconds later she has a ranked shortlist of the top 10. The 10-hour task is now a 15-minute review."

## Traction / metrics slide

**What it is for:** Show evidence that people want this and that it is progressing.

**What good looks like:**
- Specific numbers: users, revenue, growth rate, retention.
- Progress over time, not just absolute numbers. A chart showing growth over 6 months beats a single number.
- Honest framing. If it is monthly revenue, say monthly. Do not pretend it is annual.
- For early-stage companies, qualitative traction counts. Named willingness-to-pay conversations, design partners, LOIs, paid pilots.

**What to avoid:**
- Pretending monthly revenue is annual.
- Absolute numbers with no time dimension ("we have 5,000 users" without saying when).
- Vanity metrics. "100,000 page views" is meaningless if no one converts.
- Hockey-stick projections without underlying trend.
- Claiming traction the founder cannot prove.

**Important:** No traction is OK at very early stages. Many funded companies have none. **Do not invent traction.** If there is none, skip this slide and lean on customer evidence elsewhere.

**Weak:** "We have great traction. 5,000 users."

**Strong:** "We launched in March. We had 250 paying customers in May, 800 in July, 1,800 in October. Net revenue retention is 118%."

## Market / business model slide

**What it is for:** Show how the company makes money and how big the market is.

**What good looks like:**
- Clear pricing model (subscription, transaction, seat-based, per-project).
- Bottom-up TAM calculation: ACV × number of potential customers.
- Evidence the market is big enough for the kind of company you are building.

**What to avoid:**
- Top-down TAM numbers from consultant reports ("the global X market is $X billion").
- Answering only "how we make money" without "how much we could make."
- Unrealistic conversion or capture rates.

**Bottom-up TAM in four steps:**

1. Define the customer. Who specifically.
2. Annual contract value (ACV). What they pay you per year.
3. Number of potential customers. Real ones who fit the profile.
4. TAM = ACV × number of customers.

**Weak:** "We charge $100/month. The global pest control market is $25B."

**Strong:** "We charge $1,200 per restaurant per year. There are 749,404 restaurants in the US. US TAM is $899M. There are 1.5M restaurants in Europe. European TAM is $1.8B."

## Competition slide

**What it is for:** Show that you understand the landscape.

**What good looks like:**
- Direct competitors (same solution, same target).
- Indirect competitors and substitutes, including "doing nothing."
- Honest acknowledgement that competition exists.
- A clear point on how the company is different, not just better.

**What to avoid:**
- Claiming you have no competition. You always have some, even if it is "people doing it manually" or "Excel."
- Only listing direct competitors and ignoring substitutes.
- 2x2 matrices that put your company in the top right corner with no real reasoning.
- Generic "we are faster, cheaper, better" claims with no specifics.

**Weak:** "We have no real competitors."

**Strong:** "Today, restaurants handle pest control in three ways: (1) hire a monthly extermination service like Rentokil, (2) handle it in-house with traps, or (3) ignore it until they get a health violation. Our customers all started in category 2 or 3. The Rentokil customers stay with Rentokil because it is bundled into their facilities contract."

## Why now slide

**What it is for:** Show that the timing is right. Why is this company possible today when it was not possible 3 or 5 years ago, and why will the window not stay open forever?

**What good looks like:**
- A specific, recent change that made the company viable. New technology, new regulation, new market behaviour, new cost structure, new infrastructure.
- The change is real and verifiable, not a vague trend.
- The argument connects directly to the specific business, not the broader industry.
- Implicit or explicit urgency: why this is the right moment to build this, and what happens if the founder waits.

**What to avoid:**
- Vague trend statements that could be said about almost any company. "AI is changing everything." "Remote work is here to stay." "Consumers want more transparency."
- Claiming a why-now that does not actually connect to the company. If the founder is building a B2B finance tool, "AI is changing everything" is not a why-now.
- Padding the slide with multiple weak arguments. One strong why-now beats three weak ones.
- Confusing why-now with why-us. Why-now is about the market and the moment. Why-us is about the team.

**The most common categories of why-now arguments:**

- **Technology shift.** A new capability has become possible, cheap, or reliable enough to build on. ("LLM context windows just hit 1 million tokens." "Solid-state batteries dropped below $X/kWh.")
- **Regulatory change.** A new law or rule has opened or required a new market. ("PSD2 forced banks to expose APIs." "The EU AI Act requires audit trails our product provides.")
- **Cost curve.** A fundamental input cost has fallen far enough to change what is economically viable. ("GPU inference cost dropped 90% in 24 months.")
- **Behaviour change.** Users now do something at scale that they did not 5 years ago. ("Half of office work moved to async tools in 2020.")
- **Market structural change.** An incumbent has weakened, a category has fragmented, or a platform has opened. ("Twitter's API restrictions broke a generation of social tools.")
- **Infrastructure change.** A new piece of platform infrastructure has been built that the company can build on. ("Stripe Connect made marketplaces possible without payment licensing.")

The strongest why-now arguments usually combine two of these. A technology shift plus a behaviour change. A regulatory change plus a cost curve.

**Weak:** "AI is transforming every industry. We are an AI company."

**Strong:** "Two things changed in 2023. First, LLMs got good enough to read legal documents reliably; before that, the error rate was too high for regulated industries. Second, the SEC's new disclosure rules required public companies to file structured data on climate risk. We are the only product purpose-built for the intersection: AI-assisted SEC climate filings."

**When to combine why-now with competition.**

In short decks (1 to 3 minutes), the why-now argument can be folded into the problem slide or the competition slide rather than getting its own slide. The founder can say "this is possible now because of X" in one sentence. In longer decks (5 to 10 minutes), why-now usually deserves its own slide when the timing argument is central to the company's story.

The why-now slide is essential whenever the founder's pitch is "this is possible now in a way it was not before." If the company could have been built 10 years ago and just was not, the founder needs a different argument (usually team or distribution).

## Team slide

**What it is for:** Show that this team can build the company.

**What good looks like:**
- Specific achievements by specific founders. Not generic praise.
- A clear connection between each founder's background and the problem being solved.
- Evidence of seriousness (left jobs, built before, invested own money).
- For accelerator and pre-seed decks, this slide goes towards the end. Investors want to know what the company does first; the team slide answers "and can these people pull it off?"

**What to avoid:**
- Generic words ("hard-working", "dedicated", "passionate").
- Listing every advisor and mentor. **At pre-seed and first-fundraise stage, advisor and mentor logos are noise.** Investors see through it. Include advisors only when they are genuinely active in the company and recognised in the relevant domain.
- Listing the current startup as the founder's "impressive achievement."
- Vague role descriptions ("we all do a bit of everything").

**Weak:** "Jordan is an exceptionally dedicated and passionate person who is committed to solving this problem."

**Strong:** "Jordan led the payments team at Stripe from 2019 to 2022, where she shipped the Stripe Terminal SDK now used by 200,000 merchants. Before that, she was the second engineer at Brex, acquired in 2018."

## Ask slide (for investor decks)

**What it is for:** Tell the investor what you are raising, what for, and what milestones it gets you to.

**What good looks like:**
- Specific amount.
- Specific runway (in months) the round buys.
- Specific milestones the round gets the company to (revenue, hires, product, customers).
- Lead status if relevant ("we have a $500K commitment from X, raising $1M more").

**What to avoid:**
- "We are raising." Raising what? For what?
- Use of funds shown as a generic pie chart with "engineering 40%, sales 30%, marketing 20%, ops 10%." Investors do not care about the pie. They care about what milestones the money hits.
- Asking for a number that does not match the stage. Pre-seed asking for $5M is rarely the right answer.

**Weak:** "We are raising a seed round."

**Strong:** "We are raising $1.5M on a $10M cap. The round buys us 18 months of runway and gets us to $2M ARR with 6 enterprise customers signed."

## Closing slide

**What it is for:** End on a strong note and give the audience what they need to follow up.

**What good looks like:**
- A single anchor line. Often the company's vision or the problem reframed.
- Founder names and contact (email, website).
- Optionally, a thank-you.

**What to avoid:**
- "Thank you" alone with no contact details. Wasted real estate.
- Recapping every previous slide.
- Asking for the audience's questions in writing on the slide. Just say it.
