# Deck types and lengths

The same company needs different decks for different situations. The two biggest distinctions are how the deck is delivered (emailed vs. pitched live) and how long the founder has if pitching live.

This file covers both. For what each slide should contain, see `slide-by-slide.md`. For audience-specific asks, see `audience-asks.md`.

---

## Emailed deck vs. live pitch deck

These are two different artefacts. Many founders try to use the same deck for both. It does not work.

### Emailed deck (someone reads it without you)

- The reader has no narrator. The deck has to carry every part of the message on its own.
- Slides are denser. They contain enough context, data, and supporting text that the reader does not need a verbal explanation.
- **Slide titles must tell a story.** Read in sequence, the titles should form a coherent narrative on their own. The reader should be able to skim only the titles and still understand what the company does, why it matters, and where it is going. This is the single most important rule for emailed decks. Each title is a full thought, not a one-word label.
- Body copy on each slide explains the title and supports it with detail.
- Charts and visuals come with brief captions or labels. The reader should not have to guess what they are looking at.
- Length: usually 10 to 15 slides. Sometimes longer for detailed investor decks (up to 20). Anything longer than that is rare and usually a sign the deck has not been edited.

### Live pitch deck (you are pitching)

- The founder is the narrator. The slides do not deliver the message; they support it.
- Slides are minimal. Often a single image, a single line of text, a single chart, or a single anchor word.
- **The audience should not be reading the slides while the founder is talking.** If the audience is reading, they are not listening. Dense slides during a live pitch are a mistake.
- Slide titles are not load-bearing in the same way as in emailed decks because the founder is providing the verbal narrative. They still help, but the room is listening to the founder, not skimming titles.
- Length: depends on the time available (see below).

### When in doubt

Ask the founder which it is. The same content compressed onto five live-pitch slides is not the same as the same content expanded onto fifteen emailed slides. Trying to use one deck for both gives you a deck that is too dense for live pitching and too sparse for reading.

A common workable solution: build two decks from the same source. A long emailed version, and a short live version. They share the company's story but are different artefacts.

### Using an appendix for emailed decks

For emailed decks, an appendix is a useful tool. It lets the main deck stay short while giving curious readers somewhere to go for detail.

What belongs in an appendix:

- Detailed financial projections or unit economics (when relevant).
- Cohort analysis or retention curves.
- Technical architecture or product detail.
- Detailed competitive analysis.
- Deeper market sizing methodology.
- Customer case studies in full.
- Founder backgrounds in more detail than fits on the team slide.

What does not belong in an appendix:

- Anything the reader needs to see in the first 90 seconds. If it is important, it goes in the main deck.
- Filler. An appendix is not a place to dump content that did not make the cut.

Practical rules:

- Mark the appendix clearly. A divider slide that says "Appendix" is enough.
- Keep the main deck self-contained. The reader should be able to make a meeting decision without opening the appendix.
- Reference the appendix from main-deck slides when useful. ("More detail on unit economics in the appendix.")
- The appendix is for emailed decks. Live pitch decks rarely benefit from one.

## Live pitch lengths

Live pitches typically come in four standard lengths. Each one is a different design problem.

### 1-minute pitch

The hardest length. There is no time for a full slide deck. Most 1-minute pitches use 1 to 3 slides at most, or none at all.

What to cover in 60 seconds:

- Who you are and what the company does (one sentence).
- The problem (one sentence, made specific).
- The solution (one sentence).
- One piece of proof (a metric, a customer name, a demo line).
- The ask (one sentence).

That is it. Cut everything else. Founders often try to cram a 5-minute pitch into a minute and lose every second to context-setting. The right move is to ruthlessly compress.

If you are using slides, use them as anchor visuals. One per major beat. Do not animate, do not transition.

### 3-minute pitch

The most common accelerator demo day length. Typically 5 to 7 slides.

**Why 5 to 7 slides.** A demo day audience sits through dozens of pitches in a row. Realistically, they will remember 1 or 2 things from any given pitch. That means the deck should contain at most 5 to 7 ideas the founder wants the audience to remember. More slides do not help; they spread the audience's attention thinner and make every idea harder to hold on to. Pick the 5 to 7 things that matter most and build the deck around them.

A workable structure:

1. Cover slide (5 seconds, while you introduce yourself).
2. Problem slide (30 seconds).
3. Solution slide (30 seconds).
4. Demo slide or short demo video (45 seconds).
5. Traction or proof slide (30 seconds).
6. Market and business model slide (20 seconds).
7. Team and ask slide (20 seconds).

Cover slide and team/ask can be combined to save a slide. A demo video is usually stronger than describing the demo.

The thing that kills 3-minute pitches: founders try to do 5 minutes of content in 3 minutes and end up rushing. Cut content until it fits comfortably.

### 5-minute pitch

Common for slightly more in-depth investor pitches and some accelerator interviews. Typically 8 to 10 slides.

You have room for:

- A more developed problem and solution.
- A longer demo, or a demo plus screenshots.
- A traction slide with more detail.
- Separate market and business model slides.
- A "why now" or competition slide.
- A proper team slide.
- An ask slide.

Same rule as 3-minute: do not cram. The extra two minutes give you room to breathe, not to add content.

### 10-minute pitch

The classic investor meeting length. Typically 10 to 15 slides.

Now you have room for:

- A multi-slide problem framing if the problem is complex.
- A full demo.
- Detailed traction over time, not just a single snapshot.
- A real market sizing.
- A defensibility or competitive advantage slide.
- Customer evidence slides (logos, quotes, case studies).
- A clear team slide with founder backgrounds.
- A real ask with use of funds.

Even at 10 minutes, less is more. Most 10-minute pitches that go badly are 15 minutes of content rushed into 10.

## Common mistakes regardless of length

- **Spending too long on the problem.** Most audiences understand the problem in 20 seconds. Founders who spend 90 seconds on the problem in a 3-minute pitch run out of time before the demo.
- **Skipping the demo.** If you have a product, get to it as fast as possible. The demo does more work than any number of feature descriptions.
- **No ask, or a vague ask.** "We are raising" is not an ask. "We are raising $1.5M to hit $2M ARR over the next 18 months" is an ask.
- **Pitching in pairs or trios.** Do not swap presenters between slides. One founder pitches the whole thing. The others answer questions afterwards.
- **Rehearsing the deck without a stopwatch.** Time is the enemy of a live pitch. Practice with a clock running.

## Deciding what kind of deck you need

Ask the founder:

1. **Who is the audience?** Investors, an accelerator, a customer, a partner, an internal stakeholder?
2. **How will it be delivered?** Emailed for the audience to read, or pitched live?
3. **If pitched live, how long do you have?** 1, 3, 5, or 10 minutes?
4. **Is there a follow-up format?** Many investor processes use both: a short pitch deck the founder presents, followed by a longer deck the investor reads later. If so, plan both.

The answers determine almost everything else. Do not start drafting slides before you have them.
